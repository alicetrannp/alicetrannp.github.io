Who comes to us

You might have ADHD if…

📋

You've always struggled with deadlines

You start tasks fine but stall before finishing. Paperwork, emails, and admin work pile up despite your best efforts.

🔁

People call you smart but scattered

You're capable, everyone can see it, but you forget appointments, lose things, and miss details that seem obvious to others.

⏰

Time disappears on you

You sit down to do one thing and look up two hours later having done something else entirely. Or you're always, somehow, running late.

💭

Your mind never fully quiets

Racing thoughts, difficulty winding down at night, or jumping from idea to idea, even when you want to focus on one thing.

😤

You feel emotions intensely

Rejection or criticism hits harder than it should. Frustration builds fast. You wonder why small things affect you so much.

🎯

You can hyper-focus, on the wrong things

When something genuinely interests you, you can lock in for hours. But the things you have to do? Nearly impossible to start.

These aren't character flaws. They're patterns, and a clinical evaluation can tell you whether ADHD is behind them.

How it works

What to Expect at Your ADHD Evaluation

Getting evaluated for ADHD is one of the most important steps you can take toward understanding how your brain works, and why certain things have always felt harder than they should. The evaluation process here is thorough, evidence-based, and designed to get the full picture before jumping to any conclusions. Here is exactly what to expect from start to finish.

1

Book Online: No Referral Needed

Use the online booking system to schedule your first appointment. Most major insurance plans are accepted, and self-pay rates are also available. You will receive intake paperwork electronically before your visit so we can hit the ground running.

2

Your First Visit: 60-Minute Comprehensive History (Visit 1)

Your first appointment is a full hour and covers much more than just ADHD symptoms. This visit is about understanding you as a whole person. Together, we will review:

  • Your childhood development, school experiences, and early behavioral patterns
  • Work history and current daily functioning
  • Sleep, mood, anxiety, and stress levels
  • Trauma history and adverse childhood experiences
  • Medical history, prior diagnoses, and any past treatments
  • Family psychiatric history
  • Current medications and substance use history

This broad approach matters because ADHD rarely exists in a vacuum. Anxiety, depression, PTSD, sleep disorders, and thyroid conditions can all look like ADHD, or exist alongside it. A thorough history is the foundation for getting the diagnosis right.

3

Rating Scales and Paperwork: Sent After Visit 1

After your first visit, you will receive validated self-report questionnaires to complete before your follow-up, covering both your current symptoms and your childhood patterns. We will also ask for a collateral report from a partner, parent, or someone who knows you well, an outside perspective on your functioning both now and in childhood. These are not busywork; they are standardized clinical tools that add objective data to the diagnostic picture.

Depending on your medical history and treatment considerations, you may also be asked to complete:

  • An EKG (heart rhythm screening), if clinically indicated
  • Routine lab work, if you have not had bloodwork in the past six months
  • A urine drug screen
  • A controlled substance agreement, if stimulant medication is being considered

Returning these materials promptly helps keep your evaluation on track and avoids delays in reaching a diagnosis.

4

ADHD-Focused Clinical Interview: 45-Minute Follow-Up (Visit 2)

Your second appointment is dedicated specifically to ADHD. With your history already established and your rating scales in hand, this visit dives deeper into:

  • The specific pattern, frequency, and duration of your attention, impulsivity, and executive function symptoms
  • How symptoms show up across different settings: work, home, relationships, finances, driving
  • Whether your symptom profile fits the clinical criteria for ADHD
  • How ADHD symptoms interact with any co-occurring conditions identified during your first visit

This is where the diagnostic picture comes together. By combining your clinical history, validated rating scales, collateral information, and focused interview, a well-supported diagnostic conclusion can be reached.

5

Diagnosis and Treatment Plan (Visit 2 or Visit 3)

If the evaluation confirms ADHD and all necessary paperwork, lab results, and screening have been returned, a diagnosis and treatment plan are typically discussed at the end of Visit 2. Treatment options are reviewed openly and may include:

  • Stimulant medications such as Adderall, Ritalin, or Vyvanse
  • Non-stimulant medications such as Strattera (atomoxetine), Wellbutrin (bupropion), or guanfacine
  • Behavioral strategies and lifestyle modifications to support medication or serve as a standalone approach
  • Referrals for therapy, coaching, or additional testing if needed

If lab results or other paperwork are still pending at the time of Visit 2, medication will not be prescribed until everything has been received and reviewed, which may mean treatment begins at Visit 3. Patient safety always comes first, and prescribing stimulant medication requires a complete clinical picture.

When more testing helps

When Neuropsychological Testing Is Recommended

In some cases, the clinical picture is more complex than ADHD alone, and a referral for formal neuropsychological testing may be recommended. Neuropsychological testing is a comprehensive evaluation performed by a licensed neuropsychologist that goes beyond what can be assessed in a clinical interview. It involves standardized performance-based measures of memory, processing speed, executive function, sustained attention, and other cognitive abilities.

This referral is not a setback. It is a step toward getting the most precise diagnosis possible, which leads to the most effective treatment. Not every patient needs neuropsychological testing, but when it is indicated, it can be the difference between a good-enough answer and the right one.

Why the structure matters

Why Does It Take More Than One Visit?

A responsible ADHD evaluation cannot be done in a single 20-minute appointment. Rushing the process leads to missed diagnoses, wrong diagnoses, and treatments that do not work, or that make things worse. The two- to three-visit structure exists to:

  • Separate ADHD from conditions that mimic it
  • Identify co-occurring conditions that need to be treated alongside ADHD
  • Gather objective data from validated tools and collateral sources
  • Ensure safe prescribing with appropriate medical screening
  • Determine whether additional evaluation, such as neuropsychological testing, is needed
  • Build a treatment plan tailored to your specific needs, not a one-size-fits-all prescription

What If It's Not ADHD?

Sometimes the evaluation reveals that something other than ADHD is driving your symptoms: anxiety, depression, a sleep disorder, trauma, autism, or a medical condition. That is not a dead end. It is a door opening to the right treatment. And sometimes the answer is ADHD plus something else, which is actually the most common scenario in adults. Either way, you leave with clarity and a path forward.

Common questions

Frequently asked questions

How long does the evaluation take?

Your first appointment is a full 60-minute evaluation focused on your comprehensive history. This includes a detailed clinical interview and time to discuss your concerns and ask questions. A diagnosis and treatment plan are typically provided at Visit 2 or Visit 3, once your rating scales, collateral report, and any needed screening have been reviewed.

Do I need a referral?

No referral is needed. You can book directly through the online scheduling system. Alice accepts most major insurance plans and offers self-pay rates for those without in-network coverage.

Can adults really be diagnosed with ADHD for the first time?

Yes. Many adults were never identified as children, particularly women, who often present with inattentive symptoms rather than the hyperactivity that historically drove referrals. A late ADHD diagnosis is common and can be genuinely life-changing.

Do I need a neuropsychological test first?

No. Most adult ADHD diagnoses are made through a clinical interview, not a formal neuropsych battery. Neuropsychological testing is useful in specific circumstances, for example, when a learning disability or significant cognitive concern is also suspected, but it is not required.

What if I don't want to take stimulants?

That is completely respected. Non-stimulant options including Strattera (atomoxetine), Wellbutrin (bupropion), Intuniv, and Kapvay are discussed as alternatives. All treatment decisions are made collaboratively, with open discussion of the tradeoffs of each option.

Is ADHD the same as anxiety? Could it be both?

They're different conditions, though they overlap significantly in how they feel. Difficulty concentrating, restlessness, and racing thoughts appear in both. In many adults, ADHD and anxiety co-occur, meaning treatment often needs to address both. A careful evaluation helps clarify the picture. Read: ADHD vs. Anxiety →
