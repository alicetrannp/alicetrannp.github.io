ADHD

Can you have ADHD
and still get good grades?

Written & medically reviewed by Alice Tran, PMHNP-BC  ·  July 2026  ·  8 min read

The short answer is yes, absolutely. And understanding why is critical to ensuring that high-achieving individuals with ADHD are not overlooked.

The Myth That Good Grades Rule Out ADHD

One of the most persistent misconceptions about ADHD is that it is incompatible with academic success. This belief has led countless students, parents, and even clinicians to dismiss the possibility of ADHD in anyone who earns good grades. But the research tells a different story.

ADHD is associated with deficits in executive functions, including working memory, behavioral inhibition, planning, and organization, but the specific pattern of impairment varies dramatically from one individual to another. Some individuals with ADHD show pervasive impairment across multiple executive functions, while others display profound difficulty in one area but perform normally in others. Critically, some individuals with ADHD show no measurable executive function impairment at all on standardized tests.

This heterogeneity means that ADHD does not affect everyone the same way. A student with strong intellectual ability, a supportive family, or a particular passion for their subject matter may compensate for their attentional difficulties well enough to maintain high grades, even while struggling enormously behind the scenes.

What the Data Actually Show

There is no question that, on average, ADHD is associated with worse academic outcomes. A large Swedish study of over 657,000 students found that ADHD was associated with substantially lower school performance independent of socioeconomic background. A Finnish cohort study of 580,132 individuals confirmed that ADHD diagnosis at any age was associated with worse educational outcomes and a greater likelihood of pursuing vocational rather than academic education.

But averages obscure individual variation. The DSM-5 notes that "even in the absence of a specific learning disorder, academic or work performance is often impaired," the key word being "often," not "always." The diagnostic criteria for ADHD require functional impairment, but that impairment does not have to manifest as poor grades. It can appear as excessive time spent on homework, extreme stress around deadlines, inconsistent performance, difficulty with organization, or emotional exhaustion from the effort required to keep up.

The Cost of Compensation

Many high-achieving individuals with ADHD describe a phenomenon that researchers have documented: they work significantly harder than their peers to achieve the same results. They may spend hours on assignments that take classmates thirty minutes. They may rely on last-minute adrenaline to complete projects. They may develop elaborate compensatory systems, color-coded calendars, multiple alarms, rigid routines, that mask their underlying difficulties.

This compensation comes at a cost. Research shows that adolescents with ADHD have worse academic motivation at all time points compared to peers without ADHD. The constant effort required to compensate can lead to burnout, anxiety, depression, and a deep sense of being a fraud, feelings that often intensify in college or the workplace when external structures fall away.

Why This Matters for Diagnosis

The belief that good grades rule out ADHD contributes directly to delayed diagnosis, particularly in girls and women. An expert consensus statement on females with ADHD emphasized that clinicians may overlook symptoms in females because of less overt symptom manifestations and the frequent adoption of compensatory strategies. A national study found that females were diagnosed with ADHD an average of 1.7 years later than males and were more likely to receive diagnoses of anxiety or depression first.

The DSM-5 criteria do not require academic failure for an ADHD diagnosis. They require symptoms of inattention and/or hyperactivity-impulsivity that interfere with functioning or development. A student who earns straight A's but cannot maintain friendships, manage daily responsibilities, regulate emotions, or function without extreme anxiety may absolutely meet criteria for ADHD.

The Bottom Line

Good grades do not rule out ADHD. If you or someone you know is struggling with attention, organization, emotional regulation, or daily functioning, regardless of academic performance, an evaluation for ADHD is warranted. The goal of diagnosis is not to explain poor grades; it is to understand the full picture of how a person's brain works and to provide the support they need to thrive.
