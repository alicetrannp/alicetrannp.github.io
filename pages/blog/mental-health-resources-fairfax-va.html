Local Resources

Mental health resources
in Fairfax, Virginia

Written & medically reviewed by Alice Tran, PMHNP-BC  ·  July 2026  ·  7 min read

Mental health care is one of the most pressing needs in communities across the United States, and Fairfax, Virginia, is no exception. Whether you are a long-time resident or new to the area, knowing what resources are available, and understanding the evidence behind effective mental health treatment, can make all the difference.

Why Mental Health Care Matters

Anxiety disorders alone affect approximately 34% of adults during their lifetime in the United States, making them among the most common health conditions. Depression, substance use disorders, and trauma-related conditions add to the burden. Research published in JAMA confirms that these conditions are associated with significant distress and impairment, yet many people do not receive adequate treatment.

In Fairfax County, the Community Services Board (CSB) serves as the primary public-sector provider of mental health, substance use, and developmental disability services. Virginia's 39 Community Services Boards provide preadmission screening services 24 hours a day, 7 days a week, and connect residents with outpatient therapy, psychiatric services, crisis intervention, and case management.

Evidence-Based Treatments Available Locally

The gold standard for treating anxiety and depression is a combination of psychotherapy and, when appropriate, medication. Cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT) is the psychotherapy with the strongest evidence base. Meta-analyses of randomized controlled trials demonstrate that CBT produces meaningful reductions in anxiety symptoms compared to control conditions, and these effects are durable over long-term follow-up. Brief CBT interventions of six to eight sessions delivered in primary care settings have shown equivalent effects to longer-term treatment.

For medication management, selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors (SSRIs) such as sertraline and serotonin-norepinephrine reuptake inhibitors (SNRIs) such as venlafaxine extended release remain first-line pharmacotherapy for generalized anxiety disorder, social anxiety disorder, and panic disorder. These medications are associated with small to medium effect sizes compared with placebo.

Fairfax County residents can access these treatments through private practices, community mental health centers, and integrated behavioral health programs embedded in primary care offices throughout the county.

Key Local Resources

Fairfax-Falls Church Community Services Board (CSB): This is the county's public mental health agency, offering outpatient mental health services, substance use treatment, emergency services, and support for individuals with intellectual and developmental disabilities. The CSB operates the Merrifield Crisis Response Center, which provides walk-in psychiatric emergency services.

SAMHSA's Behavioral Health Treatment Locator: The Substance Abuse and Mental Health Services Administration maintains a searchable online database of licensed mental health treatment facilities throughout the United States. This tool can help Fairfax residents identify specialty mental health clinics, residential treatment centers, and outpatient programs in their area.

Primary Care Integration: Many primary care practices in Fairfax now embed behavioral health consultants who can deliver brief, evidence-based interventions for common mental health conditions. This model improves access and reduces the stigma associated with seeking mental health care.

Addressing Barriers to Care

Research published in JAMA Psychiatry has documented significant geographic disparities in access to specialty mental health care across U.S. communities. Lower-income communities often have fewer specialty mental health treatment facilities. Fairfax County, while relatively well-resourced, still faces challenges including long wait times, workforce shortages, and insurance barriers.

Digital mental health tools, including internet-delivered CBT and mobile applications, have emerged as effective supplements to in-person care. Meta-analyses show that digital CBT interventions can effectively reduce anxiety and depression symptoms compared to usual care, particularly for panic disorder, social anxiety disorder, and generalized anxiety disorder. These tools can bridge gaps while individuals wait for in-person appointments.

Taking the First Step

If you or someone you know in Fairfax is struggling with mental health concerns, the most important step is reaching out. Start with your primary care physician, contact the Fairfax-Falls Church CSB, or use SAMHSA's treatment locator to find a provider. For immediate crises, call or text 988 to reach the Suicide and Crisis Lifeline, which connects callers to trained crisis counselors 24 hours a day.

Mental health treatment works. The evidence is clear that with the right support, people recover and thrive.
