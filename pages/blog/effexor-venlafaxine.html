Effexor (Venlafaxine).
What It Does, Side Effects, and What You Need to Know About Stopping.

By Alice Tran, PMHNP-BC  ·  June 2026  ·  9 min read

Venlafaxine -- sold under the brand name Effexor -- is an antidepressant in a class called SNRIs (serotonin-norepinephrine reuptake inhibitors). It is effective for depression, anxiety, and several related conditions, and for the right patient it can be a useful medication. But it also has one of the most significant discontinuation profiles of any commonly prescribed antidepressant -- something worth understanding before you start, not after.

This article is for informational purposes only and does not replace a conversation with your prescriber. Medication decisions should always be made with a licensed provider who knows your full history.

What Is Effexor?

Venlafaxine (Effexor, Effexor XR) is an SNRI -- it blocks the reuptake of both serotonin and norepinephrine, unlike SSRIs which act primarily on serotonin alone. The addition of norepinephrine activity gives venlafaxine a somewhat different clinical profile: more energizing, more helpful for pain and fatigue, and potentially more effective for severe or treatment-resistant depression.

FDA-approved uses include:

  • Major depressive disorder
  • Generalized anxiety disorder (GAD)
  • Social anxiety disorder
  • Panic disorder

It is also used off-label for PTSD, fibromyalgia, neuropathic pain, hot flash reduction in menopause, and migraine prevention.

How It Differs from SSRIs

At lower doses (75 mg and below), venlafaxine acts primarily on serotonin -- similar in effect to an SSRI. The norepinephrine component becomes more clinically significant at higher doses (150 mg and above). This is why dose matters more with venlafaxine than with most other antidepressants: a lower dose and a higher dose are not simply more or less of the same thing.

The norepinephrine component can add:

  • More energy and motivation -- helpful when fatigue and low drive are prominent features of depression
  • Pain relief -- useful when depression co-occurs with chronic pain or fibromyalgia
  • Possible additional benefit when SSRIs alone have been insufficient

At higher doses, norepinephrine activity can also raise blood pressure -- something that should be monitored, particularly in people with pre-existing hypertension.

Side Effects

Side effects are similar to SSRIs but with a few additional considerations:

  • Nausea -- often prominent in the first 1 to 2 weeks; taking with food helps significantly
  • Sweating -- more common with SNRIs than SSRIs; can persist beyond the initial adjustment period
  • Elevated blood pressure -- dose-dependent; more relevant at higher doses or in people with hypertension
  • Increased heart rate -- mild, usually not clinically significant
  • Insomnia or vivid dreams
  • Headache
  • Dry mouth
  • Sexual side effects -- similar rate to SSRIs; reduced libido and delayed orgasm are common

The Discontinuation Problem

This is the most important section of this guide, and the one most people wish they had read before starting.

Venlafaxine has one of the shortest half-lives of any commonly prescribed antidepressant -- roughly 5 hours, with an active metabolite of about 11 hours. This means it clears the body quickly. When levels drop -- whether from a missed dose or from stopping the medication -- the brain registers the change rapidly.

The result is a discontinuation syndrome that many patients describe as among the most unpleasant experiences they have had with any medication:

  • Intense "brain zaps" -- electrical shock sensations in the head, often triggered by eye movement
  • Severe flu-like symptoms: nausea, sweating, muscle aches
  • Dizziness and difficulty walking
  • Extreme irritability or emotional instability
  • Vivid, disturbing dreams
  • Rebound anxiety that can feel worse than the original anxiety

Some people experience significant symptoms even from missing a single dose. This is not a sign of addiction -- it is a reflection of the short half-life and the brain's sensitivity to rapid changes in serotonin and norepinephrine levels. But it is real, and it is why many patients and prescribers approach venlafaxine with caution, and why it is not typically a first-line choice when other options are available.

If you are on Effexor and want to stop: do not stop abruptly. A very gradual taper -- often over months, not weeks -- is usually necessary. Some providers switch patients to a small amount of fluoxetine (Prozac) first to buffer the transition, because fluoxetine's long half-life softens the drop. Work with your prescriber to build a plan that fits your situation.

Who Is Effexor Appropriate For?

Given its side effect and discontinuation profile, venlafaxine tends to be a better fit for:

  • Patients who have tried one or more SSRIs without adequate response
  • Depression with significant fatigue, low motivation, or pain
  • Anxiety disorders (GAD, social anxiety, panic) when SSRIs have been insufficient
  • Perimenopausal women who need both mood support and hot flash reduction
  • Patients who understand and accept the discontinuation challenge going in

It is generally not the most practical first-line choice if an SSRI like sertraline or escitalopram has not been tried yet -- not because it is less effective, but because the side effect and discontinuation burden is higher, and simpler options should usually be tried first.

How Long Does It Take to Work?

Similar to SSRIs: early changes in sleep or energy may be noticeable in week 1 to 2, mood improvement typically begins around week 2 to 4, and full effect is usually evident by week 8 to 12. Dose adjustments may shift this timeline.
